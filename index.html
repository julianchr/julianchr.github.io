Birth of Relativity

What happens when something, or someone, travels near the speed of light? This is a simple question with profound consequences. And ultimately, the answer requires a new understanding of space and time altogether.

Einstein is the man responsible for this world-changing revelation. He ultimately came to the key realization that the speed of light is an unbreakable barrier. This may not seem overly exciting, however, the implications that come with this statement are huge. As explained by Brian Greene in his book The Elegant Universe, it helps to think of us traveling not through space, but through space-time. Everybody and everything in this universe travels through space-time at the same speed, the speed of light. And thus, when something travels close to the speed of light, the amount they travel through time is reduced. This applies at all speeds of travel. However, at our day-to-day speeds, we do not notice the major effects of this result. This is what the simulator will try to help with.

Time Dilation

Is time travel possible? Relatively possible at the very least.

One of the primary effects of the constancy of the speed of light is the effect of time dilation. Explained simply, time dilation states that an observer at rest will observe a moving object as moving through time more slowly than other objects at rest. This may seem counter-intuitive, however, a simple thought experiment should alleviate most concern:

Imagine two simple, equal clocks, with mirrors that bounce a particle of light from one end to the other (up and down). Every time it reaches one end, the counter goes up, thus creating a form of clock. If the speed of the particle in the clock at rest is the same (the speed of light), we should expect that at rest both clocks record the same time. However, imagine one of the clocks now moves (side to side). In order to bounce off the mirrors, the light must travel not only up and down, but also now side to side. This is obviously a longer distance to travel, and if the particle travels at the same velocity, it will clearly record a longer time to hit each mirror.

More precisely, this change in recorded time is of the Lorentz factor γ (1 / sqrt(1 - (v2 / c2))). An observer at rest will view a moving clock as moving with a rate of t / γ (with respect to their own version of t).

However, this effect is not merely limited to bouncing photons. It applies to every form of time measurement. Thus, if one were to travel to a far away place and back at close to the speed of light, the passengers would have biologically aged less than the people who remained on earth. One might imagine, if the effect is relative, and both objects can claim to be at movement, would this effect not carry over to both perspectives (i.e. both the passengers and the observers on earth can claim to be older)? However, the person that travels must accelerate and decelerate, and thus they cannot record time in the same manner. Thus, the person who travels objectively ages less than the people on earth from all perspectives.

This effect might sound a bit magical at first glance, and potentially untestable. However, we have very real experience with this phenomenon. The GPS system is one example of this. The GPS system positions its users through the use of satellites. These satellites must all be sychronized both with each other and with the ground to have accurate readings. However, in order to achieve accurate readings, the equations of relativity (and thus time dilation) had to be taken into account due to its velocity traveling in orbit.

Length Contraction

If the experience of time changes, might the experience of space as well?

This question can be answered with a very similar thought experiment as was used to understand time dilation. If the moving clock is suddenly traveling alongside an object of fixed length, one way to record the length of the object would be to record the amount of time it took for the clock to pass the object, and use that as a form of measurement. If the object is fixed, it will see the moving object pass at one time, and leave at another. This is the objective length of the rod. However, as we have seen, with time dilation, the clock at movement will record a shorter time, and thus would perceive the object as shorter by a factor of γ. This is, of course, only in the direction of travel. In other directions, perspective of space would be unchanged.

Simulator Tutorial

Some of the concepts in this simulator require some explanation.

For one, how the simulator handles movement through space. In this simulation, you control the speed and direction of movement of a rocket. Your rocket begins on Earth. To accelerate, bring your mouse into the simulation box and hold click for as long as you would like the ship to move. When you accelerate, you will see space shift, as one would if one were inside the rocket. Namely, length contraction occurs for the observer in the direction of travel.

Now, for the time dilation in the simulation. I have chosen the two planets to be the perspective of the observers. This is due to the fact that the planets are the only observers not accelerating and decelerating, and thus these equations for relative time still hold. The planets in this simulation are relatively still to one another. As you move, you should notice the time pass more slowly for the rocket than on the planets.
